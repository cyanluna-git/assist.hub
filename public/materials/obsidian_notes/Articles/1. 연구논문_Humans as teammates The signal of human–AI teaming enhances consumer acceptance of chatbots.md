A B S T R A C T   
Human–artificial intelligence (AI) teaming is a service system in which AI agents work interdependently toward 
a common goal alongside human agents. Although many consumer services rely on chatbots working with 
humans, little is known about the influence of human–AI teaming on consumers’ perceptions and use of chatbots 
in online service encounters. Using signaling theory, the present research examines whether and how human–AI 
teaming (vs. independent AI) increases consumer acceptance of chatbots. Through six scenario-based studies and 
an interview, we found that human–AI teaming can use human capabilities to endorse the effectiveness and 
authenticity of AI, leading to increased chatbot acceptance. However, this effect was not observed when AI 
capability was clear or the human service experience was negative. We contribute to information systems 
research by showing the mechanism and boundary conditions underlying the effect of human–AI teaming on 
chatbot acceptance. We also provide practical insights for managers emphasizing how human teammates in 
AI–consumer conversations can increase consumer acceptance of AI.   
1. Introduction 
Advanced artificial intelligence (AI) has the capability to become a 
human teammate rather than just a tool (Endsley, 2023). Human–AI 
teaming, defined as the integration of AI and human agents operating 
through a unified consumer interface, surpasses the performance of in­
dividual humans or AI (O’Neill et al., 2022; Rai et al., 2019; Xiong et al., 
2023). In online service encounters, AI chatbots excel in handling re­
petitive tasks without fatigue, while human employees excel in 
addressing complex issues with empathy (Daugherty & Euchner, 2020). 
Due to their complementary capabilities, these hybrid teams of humans 
and AI represent a transformative workplace trend, simultaneously 
reducing labor costs and enhancing service efficiency (Rai et al., 2019). 
While hybrid teams theoretically outperform individual humans or 
AI agents, firms are uncertain whether to disclose human teammates in 
AI–consumer conversations. On the one hand, consumers perceive AI as 
having limitations in agency and experience that cannot replace perfect 
humans; thus, human involvement may increase human transfers and 
employee workloads (Araujo, 2018; Bigman & Gray, 2018; Gnewuch 
et al., 2023). On the other hand, working with humans also makes AI 
look intelligent and reliable (O’Neill et al., 2022). Prior studies have 
investigated collaborative strategies and performance (e.g., Bayrak 
et al., 2021; Ge et al., 2021; Sinha et al., 2020; Vassilakopoulou et al., 
2023), as well as the impact of humans augmented by AI on consumer 
responses to human and system services (e.g., Gnewuch et al., 2023; 
Longoni et al., 2019; Longoni & Cian, 2022; Oleksy et al., 2023). 
However, little attention has been paid to whether human–AI teaming 
increases or decreases consumer acceptance of AI chatbots (Wei et al., 
2022). 
The above research gap raises one important question: how does 
human–AI teaming impact consumer responses to AI chatbots? We aim 
to investigate the mechanism and boundary conditions underlying the 
effect of human–AI teaming on chatbot acceptance. According to 
signaling theory, we propose that human–AI teaming increases chatbot 
acceptance by promoting chatbots’ perceived effectiveness and 
authenticity, and the signaling effect of human–AI teaming is moderated 
by AI capability and human service experience (Castelo et al., 2019; 
Jago, 2019; Karasek Iii & Bryant, 2012; Rao et al., 1999). 
Our work offers three main contributions to information systems 
research. First, it enriches the emerging literature on the influence of 
AI–human collaboration by investigating the positive effect of human­
–AI teaming on chatbot acceptance through perceived effectiveness and

2. Literature review 
2.1. AI–human collaboration 
AI–human collaboration research can be divided into two streams. 
The first stream explores collaborative strategies and performance re­
sults. Despite employees’ reluctance to work with AI that lacks social 
interaction (Savela et al., 2021; Xiong et al., 2023), managers can pro­
mote collaboration by enhancing employee decision control, AI adapt­
ability, and anthropomorphism (Harris-Watson et al., 2023; Hauptman 
et al., 2023; Sinha et al., 2020; Westphal et al., 2023). Scholars have also 
investigated the collaborative system design, collaborative weights, 
support system, risk management, and performance results (Baird & 
Maruping, 2021; Bayrak et al., 2021; Chignell et al., 2023; Endsley, 
2023; Ge et al., 2021; Haesevoets et al., 2021; Kox et al., 2021; O’Neill 
et al., 2022; Schecter et al., 2023; Seeber et al., 2020; Vassilakopoulou 
et al., 2023). 
The second research stream investigates the influence of AI–human 
collaboration (i.e., humans augmented by AI, AI supervised by humans, 
and human–AI teaming; Kannan & Bernoff, 2021; Rai et al., 2019) on 
consumer responses. Most research focuses on the type of humans 
augmented by AI. For instance, Longoni et al. (2019) showed that while 
consumers resist medical AI, they accept physician services supported by 
medical AI. Similar findings exist for recommendation, sales training, 
service, and hiring practices (Gonzalez et al., 2022; Longoni & Cian, 
2022; Luo et al., 2021; McLeay et al., 2021; Peng et al., 2022). In 
addition, AI supervised by humans does not improve people’s percep­
tion of AI capabilities (Peng et al., 2022). AI–human teaming leads 
consumers to adopt a more human-oriented communication style and 
enhances the user’s perception of the system services (Gnewuch et al., 
2023; Oleksy et al., 2023). We summarize the findings of the above 
studies in Table 1, which shows that most research focuses on the impact 
of humans augmented by AI on consumer responses to human and sys­
tem services. Studies on the effects of human–AI teaming on consumer 
responses to AI services remain scarce. 
2.2. Signaling theory 
Signaling theory revolves around the concept that buyers and sellers 
possess asymmetric information in their market interactions (Connelly 
et al., 2010). In this scenario, companies have comprehensive infor­
mation regarding product quality that consumers may lack. To sell

1994). When a target brand is combined with a credible brand, the 
alliance can provide the market with product quality information that 
the target brand cannot provide (Rao et al., 1999).

of uncertain AI capabilities. When chatbots with uncertain capabilities 
are combined with reliable humans, they can convey service quality 
information to consumers that they cannot deliver independently. We

3. Hypotheses 
3.1. Signaling effect on perceived effectiveness 
Effectiveness refers to the competence and reliability of AI in task 
completion (Johnson & Grayson, 2005). In interactive consumer service, 
consumers lack confidence in the effectiveness of AI communication and 
response (Dietvorst et al., 2015; Pelau et al., 2021). Furthermore, people 
do not trust AI as much as they trust humans because they do not un­
derstand the AI decision-making process as well as they do that of 
humans (Cadario et al., 2021; Srinivasan & Sarial-Abi, 2021; Yalcin 
et al., 2022). Therefore, compared to AI, people prefer interacting with 
human agents in service encounters (Burton et al., 2020). 
According to signaling theory, human–AI teaming can increase the 
perceived effectiveness of chatbots through the recognition and guar­
antee of human teammates. While consumers are unsure whether 
chatbots can effectively respond to and address their requests, they 
believe that the humans who team up with chatbots can (Gnewuch et al., 
2023). Human–AI teaming reinforces AI effectiveness because its signal 
shows the recognition of AI work by human teammates and provides 
human guarantees for AI behavior (Rao et al., 1999). For instance, 
working with humans makes AI appear smarter and more resourceful 
(O’Neill et al., 2022). Moreover, the signal of human–AI teaming also 
implies that human employees can improve AI-related work (Gnewuch 
et al., 2023). In summary, human–AI teaming strengthens the perceived 
effectiveness of chatbots. 
Furthermore, we posit that effective chatbots attract consumers to 
use them. Researchers have suggested that the effectiveness of AI is the 
critical factor contributing to its use (Chen et al., 2023a, 2023b; Hong 
et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2021). For instance, improving AI decisions’ ac­
curacy, explainability, and interactivity could counteract people’s 
aversion to algorithms (Mahmud et al., 2022). Therefore, consumers are 
pleased to use effective chatbots. More formally, we predict as follows: 
H1: The positive effect of human–AI teaming (vs. an independent AI 
chatbot) on chatbot acceptance is mediated by the perceived 
effectiveness. 
3.2. Signaling effect on perceived authenticity 
Authenticity refers to the degree to which the AI’s behavior reflects 
its sincere choices rather than working with scripted responses (Carroll 
& Wheaton, 2009). Due to the lack of autonomous goals and intentions, 
people tend to view AI work as unconscious and less authentic than 
human actions (Appel et al., 2020). Thus, people do not trust AI to ex­
press any purpose or valuation behind their behaviors (Jago, 2019). 
Furthermore, consumers reject the practice of AI taking on tasks that 
require sincere effort, such as symbolic product recommendations and 
moral judgments (Bigman & Gray, 2018; Granulo et al., 2020). 
Applying signaling theory, human–AI teaming enhances the 
perceived authenticity of chatbots through the social identity of human 
teammates. Although consumers might not believe that unconscious 
chatbots can understand their intentions and provide sincere feedback, 
they believe that humans who team up with chatbots can (Mou & Xu, 
2017). The signal of human–AI teaming highlights the connection be­
tween humans and AI, which shows the social identity of AI through 
human teammates (Terry et al., 1999). For instance, people tend to view 
AI work as unconscious and less authentic than human actions (Gne­
wuch et al., 2023; Jago, 2019). Thus, the signal of human–AI teaming 
could increase the social capability and service sincerity of AI (Li & 
Sung, 2021; Oleksy et al., 2023; Pelau et al., 2021). In summary, the 
signal of human–AI teaming promotes the perceived authenticity of 
chatbots. 
Building on previous research in AI, we expect that authentic chat­
bots can attract consumers to accept them. For instance, people are 
willing to use and are satisfied with robots that can think and feel (Chen 
et al., 2023a, 2023b; Hu et al., 2021; Luo et al., 2023; Schuetzler et al., 
2021; Yam et al., 2020). People can also forgive genuine robots for 
service failure (Agnihotri & Bhattacharya, 2023; Choi et al., 2021). 
Therefore, consumers are pleased to accept authentic chatbots. More 
formally, we predict as follows: 
H2: The positive effect of human–AI teaming (vs. an independent AI 
chatbot) on chatbot acceptance is mediated by the perceived 
authenticity. 
3.3. Moderating effects 
Thus far, we have presented the effect of human–AI teaming on 
chatbot acceptance from the signaling theory perspective. Although 
human–AI teaming is predicted to have a strong positive impact on 
chatbot acceptance, this signaling effect is contingent upon the ambi­
guity of AI capability and the reliability of human capability (Rao et al., 
1999). If consumers already know that the chatbot is sufficiently capable 
of accomplishing a service task, they do not need human–AI teaming to 
endorse chatbot capabilities. For example, consumers who are familiar 
with AI are more likely to use AI services than those who are not 
(Gonzalez et al., 2022). Therefore, we hypothesize as follows: 
H3: The signaling effect of human–AI teaming on chatbot acceptance 
is moderated by the AI capability. 
In addition, if consumers have a negative human service experience, 
they will realize that human service is unreliable; therefore, human–AI 
teaming would not send positive signals for AI capabilities. For instance, 
consumers who feel embarrassed about interacting with humans are 
expected to avoid human interaction in favor of AI services (Pitardi 
et al., 2022). Therefore, the effect of human–AI teaming on chatbot 
acceptance occurs only when the AI capability is unclear and the human 
service experience is not negative. More formally, we predict as follows: 
H4: The signaling effect of human–AI teaming on chatbot acceptance 
is moderated by the human service experience. 
4. Research design 
We used a mixed-method approach, including scenario-based studies 
and an interview. Scenario-based studies help us test the causal re­
lationships of our hypotheses (Lonati et al., 2018). The interview further 
enhances the credibility of our conclusions in the quantitative method 
and connects our research with the real world (Stieglitz et al., 2023; 
Venkatesh et al., 2013). Altogether, the mixed-method approach en­
riches our understanding of human–AI teaming. 
We first conducted six studies to test our hypotheses. All participants 
in the studies were recruited via Credamo (www.credamo.com), a Chi­
nese data collection platform similar to Amazon Mechanical Turk 
(MTurk) (Huang & Sengupta, 2020). To enhance the reliability of our 
data, we asked participants to respond based on their true feelings, 
tracked response time for each question, and excluded people with 
abnormal response times (Lowry et al., 2016). Before conducting all 
main experiments, we used pretests to ensure the effectiveness of 
manipulation. Across Studies 1a–1c, we consistently demonstrated the 
positive effect of human–AI teaming through various consumption sce­
narios (clothing size query, clothing choice query, resolving a delivery 
error) and measures of chatbot acceptance. We then conducted 
follow-up studies to test the mediation mechanism and boundary con­
ditions of the signaling effect of human–AI teaming. Study 2 assessed the 
mediating role of perceived authenticity and effectiveness (H1, H2) and 
excluded an alternative explanation. Studies 3a and 3b examined the 
boundary conditions of AI capability (H3) and human service experience 
(H4), respectively, and replicated the mediation mechanism of 
perceived authenticity and effectiveness. Finally, we conducted

5. Study 1 
5.1. Overview 
Study 1 supported our initial hypothesis that human–AI teaming 
increases chatbot acceptance. We manipulated human–AI teaming 
across three scenarios and used various measurements of chatbot 
acceptance to test the signaling effect of human–AI teaming. Study 1a 
focused on a utilitarian context (a clothing size query) and measured 
chatbot acceptance. Study 1b replicated the effect of human–AI teaming 
in a hedonic context (a clothing choice query) and measured the agent 
preference. Study 1c further examined the effect of human–AI teaming 
in a service recovery situation (resolving a delivery error) and measured 
chatbot acceptance by asking participants to choose between human and 
chatbot agents. 
5.2. Method 
Studies 1a, 1b, and 1c employed a two-cell, one-way (human–AI 
teaming: teaming vs. no teaming) between-subjects design. Each study 
recruited 200 participants via Credamo with marginal compensation 
(Study 1a: 74.5% female, Mage = 30.20 years, SD = 7.10; Study 1b: 
100% female, Mage = 28.26 years, SD = 7.21; Study 1c: 65.0% female, 
Mage = 28.70 years, SD = 6.76). Participants were randomly assigned to 
one of the two conditions. 
In Study 1a, the participants were asked to imagine purchasing a T- 
shirt from an online store. They were told that they had chosen one that 
was satisfactory in style, color, and material. However, they were un­
certain about the sizing, so they planned to ask the online consumer 
service. They then viewed an image of the consumer service commu­
nication interface, which contained a dialog from a chatbot. The signal 
of human–AI teaming (teaming vs. no teaming) was manipulated 
through the content sent by the consumer service chatbot. In the 
teaming condition, the participants read: “Welcome to our store. I and 
human agent FeiFei will be at your service.” In the no-teaming condition, 
the participants read: “Welcome to our store. I will be at your service.” 
Afterward, we measured participants’ chatbot acceptance by two items 
derived from prior research (“I am willing to talk with the consumer service 
chatbot” and “I will continue to use the chatbot instead of switching to a 
human agent”; 1 = strongly disagree, 7 = strongly agree; r = 0.63, p <
0.001) (van Pinxteren et al., 2019). 
In Study 1b, the participants were asked to imagine purchasing a 
blouse to match a new floral dress and needing to select one out of three 
blouses of the same style but different colors from online stores. Un­
certain about the best color choice, they planned to ask the online 
consumer service. The participants then viewed a picture identical to 
that of Study 1a, which showed a dialog from the consumer service 
chatbot. In the teaming condition, the participants read: “Welcome to our 
store. I and human agent FeiFei will be at your service.” In the no-teaming 
condition, they read: “Welcome to our store. I will be at your service.” After 
viewing the image, the participants indicated their preference to use the 
chatbot or human agents (“Would you rather use the human consumer 
service or the chatbot consumer service?”; 1 = human agent, 7 = chatbot). 
In Study 1c, the participants were asked to imagine that they had just 
bought a black camera online, but when they received it, they found it 
was silver. Therefore, they contacted the online consumer service to 
resolve the problem. The manipulation of the human–AI teaming was 
similar to Study 1a and Study 1b. The participants were then asked to 
choose between switching to human consumer service and continuing to 
communicate with the chatbot (Peng et al., 2022). 
5.3. Pretest 
We conducted three pretests to examine the validity of the human–AI 
teaming manipulation. We presented 100 adults (Study 1a: 59% female; 
Mage = 27.96 years, SD = 8.00), 100 females (Study 1b: 100% female; 
Mage = 26.35 years, SD = 7.04), and another 100 adults (Study 1c: 68% 
female; Mage = 27.18 years, SD = 8.08) drawn from Credamo with the 
same experimental manipulation as the main study. The participants 
were then asked, “Do you think that consumer service is an independent 
chatbot or a team of employees working with a chatbot?” (1 = chatbot, 7 =
human–AI teaming). 
We then used an independent samples t-test to assess our manipu­
lation check, and the results confirmed that our manipulations were 
successful (see Table 2): The participants perceived consumer service to 
be more like human–AI teaming in the teaming condition than those in 
the no-teaming condition (Study1a: t(98) = 5.29, p < 0.001, d = 1.06; 
Study1b: t(98) = 5.89, p < 0.001, d = 1.19; Study1c: t(98) = 4.17, p <
0.001, d = 0.84).

6.2. Method 
The study featured a two-cell, one-way (human–AI teaming: teaming 
vs. no teaming) between-subjects design, with 300 adults drawn from 
Credamo, who participated for monetary compensation. Four partici­
pants were excluded for taking too long; thus, the final data set consisted 
of 296 participants (61.1% female; Mage = 30.39 years, SD = 8.01). 
These participants were randomly assigned to one of the two conditions. 
The participants were asked to imagine that they had bought a new 
mobile phone from Apple’s online store but experienced a week of delay 
in the delivery. They planned to contact Apple’s online consumer service 
to inquire about the delay and the possibility of expediting the delivery. 
The participants were then shown an image of the chatbot dialog box. In 
the teaming condition, the dialog box showed: “Welcome to our store. 
Please briefly describe your problem. I and a human agent will try our best to 
help you.” In the no-teaming condition, the dialog box showed: “Welcome 
to our store. Please briefly describe your problem, and I will try my best to 
help you.” 
Afterward, we measured the participants’ chatbot acceptance (as in 
Study 1a; r = 0.78, p < 0.001) and the chatbot’s authenticity and 
effectiveness. Authenticity was measured on three seven-point items 
adapted from Jago (2019) (“Chatbots can sincerely provide services,” 
“Chatbots can provide services intimately,” “Chatbots can understand users’ 
demands”; α = 0.90). Effectiveness was measured on the scale developed 
by Longoni and Cian (2022) (“Chatbots are competent to recommend 
products for me,” “Chatbots can do a good job of product recommendation”; 
1 = strongly disagree, 7 = strongly agree; r = 0.78, p < 0.001). They 
also responded to the previously used measures of anthropomorphism 
(“The chatbot looks like a person,” “I feel like the chatbot has its own per­
sonality”; r = 0.74, p < 0.001) (Kim et al., 2016). 
6.3. Pretest 
We conducted a pretest to verify the validity of the human–AI 
teaming manipulation. One hundred adults (60% female; Mage = 32.77 
years, SD = 11.51) from the Credamo platform completed the pretest for 
monetary compensation. They were randomly assigned to one of two 
conditions and exposed to the same experimental manipulation used in

capability of AI is unclear. In other words, when consumers clearly know 
AI’s working ability and scope, they no longer need human–AI teaming

8. Study 3b 
8.1. Overview 
In Study 3b, we aimed to examine the boundary condition of the 
positive effect of human–AI teaming on chatbot acceptance by manip­
ulating a negative human service experience. We proposed that the 
positive impacts of human–AI teaming on chatbot acceptance should 
depend on the credibility of human capability (Rao et al., 1999). 
Therefore, when human capabilities are no longer trustworthy, the ef­
fect of human–AI teaming will diminish. We expected that when people 
experienced a negative human service, they would not trust human 
capabilities and react less positively to human–AI teaming. 
8.2. Method 
We randomly assigned 320 adults (67.5% female; Mage = 27.74 
years, SD = 7.57) from Credamo to one of four conditions in a 2 
(human–AI teaming: teaming vs. no teaming) ×2 (human service 
experience: negative vs. control) between-subjects design. The partici­
pants were first asked to imagine that they had bought a new mobile 
phone from Huawei’s online store and experienced a delayed delivery. 
Thereafter, in the negative human service experience condition, they 
read: “Five days later, you contacted the human agent to expedite delivery. 
After waiting for ten minutes, you were connected to a human agent. He/she 
asked you to keep waiting as there have been many delayed orders of late. 
Two days later, the logistics still had not changed, so you planned to contact 
consumer service again for an explanation and make arrangements to 
expedite it.” In the control condition, the participants did not have a 
negative human experience and just read: “Seven days later, you planned 
to contact the online consumer service to enquire about the delayed delivery 
and make arrangements to expedite it.” Next, the participants viewed an 
image showing the dialog box of the consumer service chatbot. The 
human–AI teaming manipulation was similar to that of Study 3a. 
Finally, we measured participants’ chatbot acceptance (r = 0.80, 
p < 0.001), perceived authenticity (α = 0.91), and effectiveness 
(r = 0.83, p < 0.001) with the previously used measure. 
8.3. Pretest 
We conducted a pretest to assess the validity of the negative human 
service experience manipulation. Since the control group did not expe­
rience specific human consumer service experiences, we only analyzed 
the negative experience condition. Fifty participants (68% female; Mage 
= 28.8 years, SD = 7.60) from Credamo participated in the pretest and 
received monetary compensation. Participants first read the same 
experimental materials in negative experience condition as in Study 3b; 
they then were asked: “Is the human consumer service experience negative 
or positive?” (1 = negative, 7 = positive). 
We used a one-sample t-test, and the results showed that the 
perceived human consumer service experience of participants (Mnegative 
= 2.66, SD = 1.66) was significantly below the midpoint of the scale (t 
(49) = 5.7, p < 0.001). This suggests that the negative human service 
experience manipulation was successful.

9.3. Results 
All respondents said that AI chatbots have pros and cons. Some re­
spondents declared that AI chatbots can effectively solve procedural, 
simple, and common service problems, as one respondent remarked: “AI 
chatbots can solve simple tasks.” Some respondents also stated that AI 
chatbots have the advantage of responding 24/7 and quickly, as 
mentioned by the following respondent: “I once filled in the wrong address 
information. Outside of human service hours, I directly used AI chatbot to 
modify the order address […], I don’t have to wait for the human services.” 
However, almost all respondents mentioned the incapacity of AI chat­
bots in complex and personalized problems, which is also an important 
variable in our quantitative study. Some respondents noted that “ma­
chines can undertake simple procedural tasks, but cannot do complex, spe­
cial, and personalized tasks.” 
The respondents also indicated that AI chatbots lack authenticity. 
They remarked that AI chatbots make people feel perfunctory when 
answering questions. One respondent said: “Sometimes the answers pro­
vided by the consumer service chatbot may be quite fixed, which can make 
you feel very angry [.], and it gives people a very perfunctory feeling.” In 
addition, several respondents indicated that AI chatbots do not have 
empathy and cannot truly understand their emotions. For instance, one 
respondent said: “When I communicate with an AI chatbot at this time, I feel 
that it lacks emotional ability. It doesn’t sense my emotions and attitudes; it 
doesn’t know how angry I am and how urgently I need to solve this problem 
immediately.” 
Respondents emphasized the need for AI–human collaboration. 
Some respondents indicated that human employees have some advan­
tages that AI chatbots do not possess, including solving complex and 
personalized problems and providing emotional support. One respon­
dent mentioned: “If your problem is a bit more complex, it may still require 
human intervention at the moment.” Some participants also indicated that 
employees have limited service time and slow response times. One 
respondent said: “The response from the AI chatbot is relatively fast. Unlike 
human consumer service that sometimes requires a long wait.” 
Respondents provided positive and negative experiences of 
AI–human collaboration. Some respondents believed that employees 
and AI chatbots have their strengths and weaknesses, and the collabo­
ration can enable them to leverage their strengths and better serve 
consumers. One respondent said: “In fact, for some fundamental questions, 
it may not require employees to answer them. From an ideal perspective, 
AI–human collaboration can save resources and improve efficiency.” How­
ever, some respondents also expressed negative attitudes toward 
collaboration due to poorly designed processes, for example, when AI 
chatbots could not solve problems and could not transfer to a human 
agent on time. One respondent stated: “My question was a bit more 
complicated. I just wanted to find a human employee, but there has always 
been an AI chatbot that clearly said that pressing one button can transfer to 
the credit card’s human consumer service. However, after I pressed it, it 
returned to the AI chatbot. It made me feel like I don’t even know how to press 
it.” 
Most respondents indicated that human–AI teaming can increase 
their willingness to use chatbots. They remarked that human–AI team­
ing makes them believe that chatbots are more effective and reliable, 
consistent with H1 & H2 in our study. One respondent mentioned: “I 
think the AI chatbot in the human–AI teaming has stronger abilities; it is like 
someone watching from the side. It would be better.” Another respondent 
said: “It seems like someone is in human–AI teaming. Just like humans 
endorse the AI chatbot, […], at this point, I feel that the consumer service 
chatbot is not that stupid and I may give it a try.” In addition, some re­
spondents indicated that human–AI teaming can enable human em­
ployees to supervise chatbot work, and they will also trust the chatbot 
more. One respondent said: “I think someone is monitoring the chatbot, and 
it may be a bit more intelligent when answering questions.” 
Finally, some respondents indicated that they prefer to use chatbots 
when AI capabilities are clear, which also aligns with our H3. One 
respondent said: “When AI chatbot capabilities are clear, I may directly use 
it, […]. The question I want to ask appears directly in his dialog box. I can 
click directly.” In addition, when people have had negative human ser­
vice experiences, some respondents indicated their willingness to use 
chatbots to solve problems instead of using human–AI teaming that in­
cludes employees, which supports our H4. For instance, one respondent 
said: “If an AI chatbot can solve the question, I will try to solve it with a 
chatbot because I don’t want in-depth communication with poor human 
services.” Another respondent remarked: “If the human service experience 
is not good, there is no need for any team; it is better to use the pure consumer 
service chatbot.” 
10. Discussion 
Scholars are intensely interested in how people respond to AI–human 
collaboration (Harris-Watson et al., 2023; Oleksy et al., 2023; Schecter 
et al., 2023; Sinha et al., 2020). This current research provides insights 
into the literature on AI–human collaboration and AI acceptance by 
using signaling theory to investigate the effect of human–AI teaming on 
chatbots in consumer service. Table 6 provides a summary of our results 
and main findings. 
Studies 1a–1c consistently showed that the presence of humans as 
teammates can enhance consumer acceptance of chatbots. When people 
perceive the signal of human–AI teaming, they are more likely to 
continue using chatbots. This effect was observed across different con­
sumption scenarios (Studies 1–3). Prior research on AI acceptance 
showed that people do not enjoy using AI in hedonic contexts (Longoni 
& Cian, 2022). However, our research revealed that even in hedonic 
contexts, where algorithms are mistrusted and underutilized, the pres­
ence of human–AI teaming can improve chatbot acceptance (Study 1b). 
Study 2 showed that the signal of human–AI teaming increases 
chatbot acceptance by enhancing its authenticity and effectiveness (H1, 
H2). Based on signal theory and related research, we found that 
human–AI teaming can use human capabilities to endorse the effec­
tiveness and authenticity of AI, leading to increased chatbot acceptance 
(Connelly et al., 2010; Shah et al., 2023). In line with previous research 
on AI acceptance, higher authenticity and effectiveness of chatbots 
positively affect chatbot acceptance (Jago, 2019; Kim & Duhachek, 
2020). In addition, we did not find a strong association between 
human-AI teaming and anthropomorphism in Study 2. This may be 
explained by the limited impact of endowing AI with teammates’ 
identities on anthropomorphism. Previous studies have suggested that 
the positive influence of a single anthropomorphic cue is limited; the 
improvement of anthropomorphism depends on providing sufficient 
anthropomorphic features (Schanke et al., 2021). 
Study 3a confirmed the moderating effect of AI capability on the 
relationship between human–AI teaming and chatbot acceptance (H3). 
Our findings showed that people were more likely to accept the chatbot

10.1. Theoretical contributions and implications 
This research contributes to the information systems literature in 
several ways. First, we have enriched the literature on the influence of 
AI–human collaboration by examining how the signal of human–AI 
teaming increases chatbot acceptance through perceived effectiveness 
and authenticity. Previous studies have focused on AI support to facili­
tate human work (Longoni et al., 2019; Longoni & Cian, 2022; Peng 
et al., 2022) and the influence of AI–human teaming on system work 
(Gnewuch et al., 2023; Oleksy et al., 2023). Building upon this foun­
dation, our research advances the understanding by positing that 
humans, when acting as teammates, can also promote AI work. Specif­
ically, we demonstrate that human–AI teaming sends the AI quality 
signals of effectiveness and authenticity to increase chatbot acceptance. 
In doing so, we reveal that human capabilities positively endorse the 
cognitive effectiveness and affective authenticity of AI capabilities 
through human–AI teaming. 
Second, this research contributes to the literature on collaborative 
strategy and performance by considering AI and human factors that 
facilitate AI–human collaboration. Previous research has explored the 
resistance and impetus to AI–human collaboration (Harris-Watson et al., 
2023; Savela et al., 2021) and suggested autonomy, human, task, team, 
training characteristics, and trust-building strategies to increase indi­
vidual and team performance (Kox et al., 2021; O’Neill et al., 2022; 
Seeber et al., 2020). Besides this literature, we argue that the ambiguity 
of AI capabilities and the reliability of human capabilities also impact 
the validity of human–AI teaming signals (Levin & Levin, 2000; Rao 
et al., 1999). These insights expand our understanding of the collabo­
rative strategy of human–AI teaming. 
Lastly, the current research brings new insights to the literature on AI 
chatbot acceptance by identifying the unexplored antecedent variable of 
human–AI teaming. Previous work has suggested that chatbot features 
such as self-learning ability, conversational capability, anthropomor­
phism, autonomy, interpretability, empathy, and emotional expression 
(Borau et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2023a, 2023b; Grimes et al., 2021; Han 
et al., 2023; Hong et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2021; Schanke et al., 2021; 
Schuetzler et al., 2021; Seeger et al., 2021), as well as task types such as 
objectively, utilitarianism, and competence (Castelo et al., 2019; 
Longoni & Cian, 2022; Peng et al., 2022), influence consumers’ accep­
tance of AI. Particularly in collaborative designs, scholars have sug­
gested that humans augmented by AI positively affect the acceptance of 
collaborative systems (Gonzalez et al., 2022; Longoni et al., 2019; 
Longoni & Cian, 2022). Furthermore, we examine and find that the 
chatbot feature of human–AI teaming also improves perceived effec­
tiveness and authenticity, inducing increased chatbot acceptance. 
10.2. Implications for practice 
Our findings offer implications for managers regarding how to 
employ human–AI teaming in online service encounters. First, as natural 
language processing grows and chatbots become an extension of the 
human team, managers must align their AI team members with their 
human team members. Our findings indicate that consumer perceptions 
of AI capabilities can be enhanced if managers show the signals of 
human–AI teaming. Even though human employees may not directly 
engage with consumers, they play a pivotal role as the conduit fostering 
trust in AI capabilities. To amplify consumer acceptance of AI, managers 
can strategically highlight human employees’ collaborative spirit, 
teamwork, and active involvement in AI-consumer interactions. It’s 
crucial to frame the relationship between humans and AI as a genuine 
partnership, emphasizing equality, as positioning humans as superior to 
AI may inadvertently steer consumers towards preferring human ser­
vices. This shift towards a unified human–AI teaming enhances con­
sumer trust and propels AI’s acceptance and integration within the team 
dynamic. 
Second, managers should refrain from employing human–AI teaming 
strategies when AI capabilities are insufficient. The first crucial step 
involves an accurate assessment of AI’s task-handling range. Managers 
should consider implementing human–AI teaming to enhance AI adop­
tion only when AI can effectively tackle a proposed task. However, a 
careful approach is necessary, as relying on human–AI teaming without 
proper evaluation may create expectation violation. This approach can 
lead to disappointment if AI fails to meet consumer demands. Therefore, 
managers must exercise prudence, aligning consumer expectations with 
actual AI capabilities to avoid potential dissatisfaction. 
Third, we suggest that the signaling effect of human–AI teaming 
depends on the ambiguity of AI capabilities. If consumers trust AI 
enough to respond to their requests, there is no need for a human 
endorsement of AI capabilities. The key lies in creating a clear and 
distinct division of labor between humans and AI, allowing consumers to 
swiftly decide whether to interact with the AI or seek assistance from a 
human. To illustrate, savvy managers can strategically communicate the 
specific tasks at which AI excels within consumer service dialogues, 
effectively capturing people’s attention and encouraging them to 
leverage the capabilities of AI. This practical approach ensures that 
consumer expectations align well with what AI can do, contributing to 
more effective and satisfying interactions between humans and AI. 
Finally, we suggest that the signaling effect of human–AI teaming 
depends on the reliability of human capabilities. Specifically, negative 
encounters with human services emerge as potent factors capable of 
attenuating the positive signaling associated with human capabilities. A 
concrete illustration of this phenomenon is observable in situations 
characterized by consumer discomfort, such as privacy issues. In such 
scenarios, consumers tend to prefer interactions with AI agents as 
opposed to engaging in human–AI teaming. The rationale behind this 
inclination lies in the potential for human–AI teaming in these sensitive 
contexts to yield suboptimal and unfavorable experiences. In navigating 
the landscape of human–AI teaming, a reasonable balance between 
promoting AI strengths and handling human interactions emerges as an 
indispensable factor in optimizing overall effectiveness and consumer 
satisfaction.

10.3. Limitations and future research directions 
Our research also has some limitations. First, in our scenario-based 
studies, we implemented various measures such as attention checks, 
response time limits, and the prohibition of repeated responses to 
enhance data quality. However, it is important to note that these efforts 
do not ensure flawless data. Furthermore, despite incorporating an 
interview study to bolster the external validity of our scenario-based 
findings, the absence of actual behavioral data hinders our ability to 
confirm our hypotheses. Future research could analyze real consump­
tion data or conduct field experiments to test the teaming signaling ef­
fect. Second, although we investigated the moderating roles of AI work 
capabilities and human service experience, future research could 
explore other boundary conditions or moderating variables. For 
instance, the degree of linkage between AI and humans may affect the 
credibility of collaborative signals (Levin & Levin, 2000). Specifically, 
interdependent teaming enhances the perceived capabilities of AI, while 
independent teaming inhibits the positive signals of human capabilities. 
Third, although we adopted signaling theory to explain the effect of 
human–AI teaming on AI acceptance and identified the critical media­
tors of authenticity and effectiveness, theoretical speculations about 
how human–AI teaming affects AI acceptance warrant additional 
research. One possible mechanism is in-group identification. Human–AI 
teaming blurs the boundary between AI and humans, which may in­
crease consumers’ in-group identification with AI and reduce the risk of 
using AI (Castelo et al., 2019). 
11. Conclusions 
Our research investigates the mechanism and boundary conditions 
underlying the effect of human–AI teaming on chatbot acceptance. 
Drawing upon signaling theory, we have proposed and empirically 
found that the signal of human–AI teaming increases chatbot acceptance 
by enhancing the authenticity and effectiveness of the chatbot and that 
this teaming signaling effect depends on the ambiguity of AI capabilities 
and the reliability of human capabilities. Thus, the presence of humans 
as teammates can enhance, rather than reduce, consumer acceptance of 
chatbots. Complementary human–AI teaming will result in enhanced 
consumer service experiences and reduced labor costs.

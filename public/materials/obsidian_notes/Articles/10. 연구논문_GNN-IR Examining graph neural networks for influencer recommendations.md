With the notable growth of the Internet, a number of platforms have emerged and attracted an enormous number 
of users. Based on the impact of these platforms, some ‘inﬂuencers’ are highlighted. These inﬂuencers wield 
signiﬁcant power, shaping consumer behavior. This inﬂuence spawned the concept of inﬂuencer marketing, 
where companies leverage these personalities to advertise their products. YouTube stands out as a prominent 
platform in this trend. However, considering the limited number of inﬂuencers and their concepts, the majority 
of companies, which hope to conduct their marketing campaigns with inﬂuencers face challenges in identifying 
suitable inﬂuencers for their campaigns. With this trend, we introduce GNN-IR, a graph neural network for 
inﬂuencer recommendation, based on the connections between companies and inﬂuencers of YouTube, one 
of the largest content platforms. In developing GNN-IR, we adopted a data-driven methodology utilizing a 
meticulously curated dataset collected in-house. Our dataset comprises a total of 25,174 relationship entries 
between advertisers and inﬂuencers, involving 1,886 distinct advertisers and 3,812 unique YouTube inﬂuencers. 
It encompasses diverse data modalities, including images, text, and assorted metadata. The data was sourced 
from two primary platforms: YouTube and ugwanggi. Ugwanggi provided valuable insights into the relationships 
between advertisers and inﬂuencers via their information. Meanwhile, YouTube oﬀered more comprehensive 
and detailed inﬂuencer-centric information. We employed PyTorch Geometric to construct a bipartite graph 
representing interconnected data. Our recommendation system operates via link prediction, suggesting the Top-
k inﬂuencers to advertisers based on the calculated connection probability between nodes. To assess GNN-IR’s 
performance, we employed a range of evaluation metrics. For link prediction, we measured Accuracy, Precision, 
Recall, and F1-score. Additionally, in the recommendation phase, we evaluated Precision@k, Recall@k, and 
F1-score@k. Using GNN-IR and incorporating proﬁle images from YouTube, keyword features, metadata, and 
sentiment gleaned from YouTube comments, we achieved Precision levels of 96.51% at k=1 and 93.68% at 
k=10. Based on the experimental results, several implications and limitations are presented. The collected 
dataset is publicly available at https://github .com /dxlabskku /GNN -IR .git.

2. Related work
2.1. Inﬂuencer marketing on social media
As social networking services (SNS) have gained attention, busi-
nesses are increasingly utilizing these services not only for casual user 
interaction but as pivotal channels for inﬂuential marketing strate-
gies (Farivar and Wang, 2022). Within this context, inﬂuencer market-
ing has emerged as a crucial component of contemporary marketing 
practices. Inﬂuencers utilize their specialized knowledge to consistently 
create engaging content, fostering active engagement with their follow-
ers (Zhu et al., 2019; Venciute et al., 2023). These experts boast substan-
tial follower bases, eﬀectively setting trends in the online sphere (De 
Veirman et al., 2017). In line with this, there is a notable shift in 
how followers perceive inﬂuencers, viewing them as aspirational ﬁg-
ures with signiﬁcant sway over their purchasing decisions (Barta et al., 
2023). The products endorsed by inﬂuencers often experience height-
ened demand, highlighting the growing importance of inﬂuencer mar-
keting in shaping consumer behavior.
Inﬂuencers possess a pivotal attribute that signiﬁcantly shapes con-
sumer purchasing behavior: trustworthiness (Farivar and Wang, 2022). 
This quality substantially bolsters an inﬂuencer’s credibility and per-
suasive impact. When consumers perceive an inﬂuencer as trustworthy,

2.2. Recommendation system
Recommendation systems are generally classiﬁed into three types: 
content-based ﬁltering, collaborative ﬁltering, and hybrid ﬁltering (Hu 
et al., 2023; Isinkaye et al., 2015). Content-based ﬁltering techniques 
rely solely on content to make their recommendations (Das et al., 2017). 
These algorithms suggest items similar to those previously chosen by 
the user (Hwang et al., 2023). Collaborative ﬁltering techniques, on the 
other hand, use interaction data between users and items, which can 
be further categorized into memory-based ﬁltering and model-based 
ﬁltering. Memory-based ﬁltering operates by assessing similarity (Na-
garnaik and Thomas, 2015). When it assesses similarity between users, 
it is termed user-based. If it focuses on item similarity, it is referred to 
as item-based. To measure similarity levels, cosine similarity, Euclidean 
distance, and Pearson’s correlation are commonly used in these calcu-
lations (Singh et al., 2020).
Model-based ﬁltering methods utilize machine learning algorithms 
to compute underlying characteristic values for users or items. One of 
the representative approaches is latent factor modeling, which involves 
matrix factorization (Koren et al., 2009). Through matrix factorization, 
both users and items are projected into a latent factor space. Their in-
teraction is then represented by the dot product of these projections. 
Other model-based ﬁltering methods encompass Bayesian networks and 
Neural Networks (Isinkaye et al., 2015). These approaches leverage dif-
ferent techniques within machine learning to enhance recommendation 
systems.
Collaborative ﬁltering confronts several challenges including the 
cold start issue, sparsity, and scalability (Polatidis and Georgiadis, 
2017; Sharma et al., 2022). The cold start problem emerges with new 
users, making recommendations challenging due to the absence of prior 
user information (Behera et al., 2020). Sparsity occurs when there are 
more users than items, resulting in a sparse matrix due to numerous 
unassessed items by users. Scalability becomes a concern in real-world 
scenarios due to the immense number of users and items, leading to 
high computational costs (Srivastava et al., 2020). To address these lim-
itations, a hybrid ﬁltering approach was introduced, aiming to mitigate 
the drawbacks of both content-based and collaborative ﬁltering. Hybrid 
ﬁltering combines multiple recommendation system algorithms of dif-
ferent types (Isinkaye et al., 2015), oﬀering a more comprehensive and 
eﬀective recommendation strategy.
2.3. Graph-based recommendation system
With the rapid development of deep learning, the graph neural net-
work (GNN) is introduced for a series of recommendation systems, de-
pending on neural networks that utilize graph structures. GNN present 
the ability to capture not only just user-item relationships but also 
the inherent graph structure within the data. A graph is denoted as 
G=<V, E>, where V and E are the set of nodes and edges in the 
graph, respectively. When v∈V, the neighbors of node v are deﬁned 
as N(v)={𝑢∈𝑉|(𝑣,𝑢) ∈𝐸}. These relationships within the graph play 
a crucial role in item recommendations (Wu et al., 2022).
The GNN-based recommendation system includes both general and 
sequential recommendation methodologies. In general recommenda-
tion, particular suggestions are presented, based on the probability of 
choosing a speciﬁc node, where interactions between diﬀerent types of 
nodes are often examined as edges in a bipartite graph. Sequential rec-
ommendation, on the other hand, learns from time-stamped sequences 
by addressing historical interactions between nodes based on time-
oriented data (Jiang et al., 2022).

Graph embedding methods represent another prevalent approach in 
link prediction. These methods involve embedding graph nodes into a 
lower-dimensional space, enabling the prediction of connection proba-
bilities between nodes by assessing the similarity of node embeddings. 
This technique capitalizes on lower-dimensional representations to in-
fer potential connections within the graph structure.
Recent developments in graph embedding methodologies present 
several advanced techniques such as DeepWalk, Node2vec, and Large-
scale information Network Embedding (LINE) (Tang et al., 2015). Deep-
Walk generates embeddings by utilizing random walks (Perozzi et al., 
2014), where it generates random walks and presents node embeddings 
via Word2vec. Node2vec, an enhancement over DeepWalk, introduces 
speciﬁc adjustments utilizing hyperparameters p and q (Grover and 
Leskovec, 2016). In detail, p signiﬁes the probability of returning to 
the prior node, impacting breadth-ﬁrst search (BFS), while q examines 
depth-ﬁrst search (DFS).
LINE, on the other hand, integrates ﬁrst-order and second-order 
proximity to generate node embeddings (Tang et al., 2015). However, 
as complex networks across various domains evolve, there is a grow-
ing trend toward depending on diverse node and edge features to en-
hance link prediction tasks. While a number of embedding methods 
mainly depend on topological information to forecast link existence, 
text-associated DeepWalk (TADW) diﬀers by integrating textual fea-
tures alongside network structures (Yang et al., 2015), leveraging the 
foundation of random walk.
Moreover, several techniques like tri-party deep network represen-
tation algorithm (TriDNR), GraphSAGE, RBM and AANE utilize net-
work feature data when generating node embeddings (Pan et al., 2016; 
Huang et al., 2017; Le and Mikolov, 2014; Hamilton et al., 2017; Wang 
et al., 2016). These approaches present the evolution toward integrating 
richer feature sets for improving node representation within complex 
networks.
2.5. Link prediction in bipartite graph
The recommendation system serves as an application scenario for 
link prediction, especially in the context of bipartite graphs commonly 
found in real-world scenarios, particularly in recommendation systems. 
A bipartite graph is symbolized as G=<X, Y, E>, as presented in Fig. 1. 
X and Y denote sets of nodes of distinct types, while E represents the 
set of edges connecting these nodes. Every edge (x, y) can only connect 
a node from set X to a node in set Y or vice versa (Daud et al., 2020). 
In a bipartite graph, link prediction operates as follows: Considering 
a node pair (x, y) belonging to diﬀerent types, where k signiﬁes the 
hop distance between them. Γ𝑘(𝑥) is the k-hop neighbors of node x, 
while Γ𝑘(𝑦) is the k-hop neighbors of node y. Therefore, in a bipartite 
graph, the set of common neighbors between node x and y, represented 
as Γ𝑘(𝑥) ∩Γ𝑘(𝑦) is null. When k is even, the initial and terminal node 
types are identical in the path. Lastly, odd k values hold signiﬁcance 
when recommending y to x within the recommendation system.
2.6. Bipartite link prediction in various domains
Link prediction in bipartite graphs ﬁnds applications across diverse 
domains such as music, hospitality, e-commerce, and the movie indus-
try. In the realm of music recommendation systems, bipartite graphs 
aid users in discovering appealing music, fostering loyalty towards as-
sociated enterprises (Zhang et al., 2020). For instance, leveraging data 
from the Shrimp community, a music content discovery platform in 
China, a dataset encompassing 76 user nodes, 16,156 music nodes, and 
24,024 edges was employed to construct a bipartite graph. Link predic-
tion tasks in this scenario utilize the similarity features among diﬀerent 
node groups.
Similarly, in the hotel recommendation domain, the objective is to 
assist travelers in ﬁnding suitable accommodations based on insights de-
rived from previous travelers’ reviews (Kaya, 2020). Using data sourced 
from TripAdvisor .com, a dataset comprising 6,928 hotels, 47,776 cus-
tomers, and 152,637 links was utilized to create a bipartite graph 
encompassing two node types: customer and hotel. This framework en-
ables the prediction of potential links between customers and hotels to 
facilitate improved recommendations for travelers.
An e-commerce recommendation system was developed to stream-
line the search process by promptly suggesting relevant items to users 
based on their purchase history (Liu, 2022). This system relied on the 
FilmTrust and Epinions datasets, fundamental for testing recommenda-
tion algorithms.
FilmTrust constitutes a collection of user-item ratings sourced from 
the Film Trust website, encompassing 35,497 ratings, 1,642 users, and 
2,071 ﬁlms. Utilizing this dataset, a bipartite graph was formed, linking 
user nodes with ﬁlm nodes to represent their relationships. Epinions, 
which is sourced from Epinions .com, an e-commerce platform, com-
prises 82,461 ratings, 6,000 users, and 12,000 items. Constructing a 
bipartite graph using user and item nodes facilitated the depiction of 
their connections, signifying user-item ratings. These instances illustrate 
how bipartite graph recommendation tasks transcend speciﬁc domains, 
demonstrating their adaptability across various industries.

tions. Furthermore, to facilitate comparison across channels, we con-

to integer type.

Our GNN-IR structure demonstrated its optimal performance when 
employing GraphSAGE as the backbone. The operating structure of 
GraphSAGE is as follows:
• GraphSAGE (Hamilton et al., 2017) represents a prominent graph 
neural network employed for generating node embeddings, cap-
turing concise vector representations of nodes within a graph. Di-
verging from the conventional method of considering every neigh-
bor node of a target node, GraphSAGE updates multiple neigh-
boring nodes using sampling techniques, eﬃciently reducing time 
complexity during node embedding computations. Notably, Graph-
SAGE operates through inductive learning, concentrating solely on 
nodes and edges within the training dataset rather than the entire 
graph. This approach enables adaptability to graph modiﬁcations, 
accommodating additional nodes and edges even after initial train-
ing, rendering it adept at handling unseen data. Its versatility in 
real-world network applications remains a signiﬁcant advantage. 
Considering our research context—marked by a continuous inﬂux 
of inﬂuencers to YouTube and the establishment of new commer-
cial relationships between advertisers and YouTube inﬂuencers—
GraphSAGE’s proﬁciency in inductive learning becomes particu-
larly pertinent. Furthermore, GraphSAGE oﬀers three aggregate

3.4.6. Link prediction
The trained GNN-IR model serves to predict whether individual 
edges denote interactions between advertisers and inﬂuencers. For this 
purpose, a straightforward linear layer conducts a dot product opera-
tion between two node embeddings, generating a probability score for 
the connection between these nodes. A prediction of 1 signiﬁes an edge 
representing an advertiser-inﬂuencer interaction, while 0 denotes oth-
erwise. During evaluation, we performed link prediction on our test 
dataset and assessed the model’s performance using common metrics 
such as Accuracy, Precision, Recall, and F1-score.
3.4.7. Recommendation
We performed the recommendation task individually for each ad-
vertiser within the test dataset. The edge data for the target advertiser 
comprised both positive edges—representing actual connections—and 
negative edges, which indicated non-existent connections. For the Top-k
recommendation task, the system generated a recommendation list sug-
gesting k edge pairs with the highest probabilities of being connected. 
Our evaluation metrics for this task were Precision@k, Recall@k, and 
F1-score@k.
3.5. Baseline models
Graph learning serves not only as a machine learning model but also 
as a graph neural network. Therefore, in our study, we considered both 
traditional machine learning (ML) models and GNN models as base-
line approaches. In the machine learning approach leveraging graph 
structures, we generated node embeddings that incorporated both node 
features and their neighboring nodes. This involved creating node em-
beddings by aggregating the embedding values of nodes corresponding 
to existing edges within our dataset, assigning a label of 1 to these 
edges. For GNN-IR learning, we maintained a 2:1 ratio of negative edges 
to positive edges. To retain this ratio, we artiﬁcially created negative 
edges, making them twice as numerous as positive edges, and labeled 
them as 0. We then combined these pairs of node embeddings to train 
ML models. The resulting dataset comprised 25,174 instances labeled as 
1 and 50,348 instances labeled as 0. We randomized this input data and 
split it into an 80-20 train-test ratio using the Sklearn library. Our cho-
sen models for experimentation included Random Forest (RF), Support 
Vector Classiﬁer (SVC), Naive Bayes (NB), K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN), 
and Extreme Gradient Boosting (XGBoost).
• RF (Breiman, 2001; Lee et al., 2023a) is an ensemble model to 
address the overﬁtting problem, which is a drawback of decision 
trees. This ensemble technique utilizes bagging, comprising boot-
strapping and aggregating methods. Bootstrapping involves sam-
pling with replacement, while aggregating utilizes majority voting 
to combine the predictions of numerous trees generated through 
bootstrapping.
• SVC (Hearst et al., 1998) is designed for classiﬁcation tasks using 
linear boundaries. It identiﬁes a separating line that maximizes the 
margin distance between the line and the closest elements.
• NB (Rish et al., 2001) is a statistical classiﬁcation technique 
grounded in Bayes’ theorem. After computing posterior probabil-
ities, the classiﬁcation occurs by assigning the label value with the 
highest probability.
• KNN (Peterson, 2009; Lee et al., 2023b) identiﬁes k neighboring 
elements closest to the current element and classiﬁes based on 
the majority label among these neighbors. Commonly, Euclidean 
and Manhattan distances are employed for distance calculations in 
KNN.
• XGBoost (Chen and Guestrin, 2016; Park, 2023) is operated on de-
cision trees and complements previous models through a gradient-
based technique. It works by iteratively constructing a model that 
minimizes a speciﬁed loss function.
We utilized several graph-based baseline models, including Graph 
Convolution Network (GCN), Graph Attention Network (GAT), basic 
Graph Neural Network (GNN), Uniﬁed Message Passing Model (UniMP), 
Slot-based Message Passing for Heterogeneous Graphs (SlotGAT), and 
Neighborhood-enriched Contrastive Learning (NCL). Each model oﬀers 
distinct architectures and mechanisms for leveraging graph structures 
in learning and inference tasks.
• GCN (Kipf and Welling, 2016) utilizes a characteristic where it em-
ploys the same weight matrix for aggregating neighboring nodes 
without distinguishing between its own weight matrix and those of 
neighboring nodes. Additionally, it considers the degrees of neigh-
boring nodes during the normalization process.
• GAT (Veliˇckovi´c et al., 2018) is a method that assigns weights to 
nodes based on attention scores, prioritizing more important nodes 
while not considering all neighboring nodes equally signiﬁcant.
• GNN (Scarselli et al., 2009) combines the weight matrix from the 
previous layer of the target node with the average weight matrix 
of embedding values from neighboring nodes’ previous layers. It 
proceeds by passing this combined information through the ReLU 
activation function for neighborhood aggregation.
• UniMP (Shi et al., 2021) integrates node feature propagation with 
labels and utilizes masked label prediction. It employs a technique 
where it randomly masks input label information before prediction, 
enhancing the model’s learning process.
• SlotGAT (Zhou et al., 2023) utilizes a method of message pass-
ing technique, assigning individual slots for each node type to 
disentangle semantic complexities among diﬀerent nodes. It is fur-

sentiment and image features, both deemed signiﬁcant in GNN-IR. All

from 89.51% to 88.72%, Precision from 87.09% to 86.02%, Recall from

this feature in inﬂuencing model performance. However, the removal of 
the NCSR led to a decrease in Accuracy from 89.51% to 88.59%, Preci-
sion from 87.09% to 82.49%, and the F1-score from 83.64% to 83.00%. 
Despite a stable Recall, the substantial decline in Precision underscores 
the feature’s signiﬁcance in a recommendation system.

5. Discussion and conclusion
We aimed to leverage data-driven approaches to recommend suit-
able inﬂuencers to commercial advertiser companies. We incorporated 
a diverse range of features, including image, sentiment, text, and meta-
data, leveraging feature extraction techniques to enhance their quality. 
Our experiments focused on optimizing the GNN backbone method to 
identify the most eﬀective approach.
In our investigation, GNN-IR, our proposed approach, utilizing 
GraphSAGE as its backbone, emerged as the most successful. It achieved 
an Accuracy of 89.51%, Precision of 87.09%, Recall of 80.45%, and 
an F1-score of 83.64% in link prediction, outperforming other baseline 
models across all metrics. Notably, GNN-IR excelled in Precision@k, 
showcasing the highest performance in this aspect.
Moreover, in our ablation study focusing on GNN-IR, we found that 
the negative sentiment feature signiﬁcantly contributed to achieving 
high performance. Similarly, while the image feature played a substan-
tial role, its impact was slightly lower than that of negative sentiment. 
Notably, combining these two features yielded improved performance, 
underscoring the synergy between negative sentiment and image fea-
tures in enhancing recommendation outcomes.
This research can contribute to academia by expanding the domain 
of recommendation systems through data-driven approaches. While 
the area of advertiser-inﬂuencer relationships within recommendation 
systems remains relatively unexplored, we demonstrate its potential 
applications. Signiﬁcantly, we introduced a set of features in our ex-
periments, mainly including sentiment and image features for node 
representations–features rarely explored in prior research. This exper-
imental setup and results can improve our understanding of feature 
usefulness, highlighting the signiﬁcance of these rarely utilized features. 
By speciﬁcally incorporating keyword and sentiment features, we en-
sured the validation of trustworthiness—an integral element for success 
in inﬂuencer marketing. By mirroring the industry’s actual inﬂuencer 
decision-making processes, the GNN-IR model can oﬀer more practical 
and tailored recommendation outcomes suited to the dynamic inﬂu-
encer marketing landscape.
Moreover, our research can hold practical implications by present-
ing the feasibility of making recommendations based solely on disclosed 
information and historical connections within advertiser-inﬂuencer re-
lationships. This capability addresses the challenge of selecting ap-
propriate inﬂuencers in modern inﬂuencer marketing, providing man-
agerial insights for companies to enhance proﬁtability by strategically 
leveraging inﬂuencers.
In addition, our ﬁndings underscore the eﬀectiveness of GNN-IR 
with GraphSAGE as its backbone. GraphSAGE’s capability for induc-
tive learning, particularly in handling unseen data, renders GNN-IR 
suitable for managing the complexity of real-world networks. Further-
more, due to GNN-IR’s capability to autonomously select features, the 
model exhibits scalability across various studies in diverse ﬁelds. While 
our study primarily focused on YouTube, inﬂuencer marketing actively 
occurs across various social platforms like Instagram, Facebook, and 
TikTok. Hence, future research exploring these platforms could provide 
valuable insights and extend the applicability of our ﬁndings to diverse 
social network contexts.
6. Future work
In our study, we developed a recommendation model employing 
graph neural network techniques to suggest inﬂuencers to advertis-
ers. This model surpasses conventional approaches that rely solely on 
human judgment by factoring in inﬂuencer authenticity and trustwor-
thiness. Our methodology considers various advertiser categories like 
beauty & makeup, fashion & clothing, food & beverage, gaming, and 
IT/devices. We utilize YouTube comments’ sentiment and keyword fea-
tures as criteria for assessing trustworthiness. However, we acknowl-
edge a limitation in validating our computational results against expert 
opinions. Particularly in sensitive niches such as health, ﬁnance, or 
social domains, inﬂuencers are required to shoulder a higher responsi-
bility in ensuring the trustworthiness of product promotions compared 
to other sectors. Hence, in future endeavors, we propose a hybrid ap-
proach, amalgamating computational analysis with expert evaluation. 
This would shed light on whether computational ﬁndings align with 
expert assessments, ensuring the authenticity and trustworthiness of in-
ﬂuencers, especially in critical sectors.

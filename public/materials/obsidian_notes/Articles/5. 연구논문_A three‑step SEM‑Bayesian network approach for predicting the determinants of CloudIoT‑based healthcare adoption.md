Abstract‚ÄÉ Adopting the CloudIoT-based healthcare para-
digm provides various prospects for medical IT and con-
siderably enhances healthcare services. However, compared 
to the advanced development of CloudIoT-based healthcare 
systems, their usage is relatively low among healthcare 
professionals in hospitals. Thus, a study on the healthcare 
professionals acceptance and adoption of CloudIoT-based 
healthcare is critical. This study explores factors influenc-
ing healthcare professionals adoption of CloudIoT-based 
healthcare solutions by extending the Unified Theory of 
Acceptance and Use of Technology 2 model. Data was col-
lected from 300 healthcare professionals in Jordan through 
an online questionnaire developed using the ¬≠Google¬ģ form 
application. The Bayesian Network and Structural Equation 
Modeling techniques were used to analyze and validate the 
proposed model. The results revealed that the healthcare 
professional‚Äôs behavioral intention is directly affected by 
seven factors: performance expectancy, effort expectancy, 
facilitating conditions, habit, trust, privacy, and security. 
The results also indicate that trust mediates the influence of 
privacy, facilitating conditions, and performance expectancy 
on behavioral intention. The research results will aid Clou-
dIoT service providers, healthcare organizations, designers, 
and developers by offering comprehensive knowledge of the 
significant factors of CloudIoT-based healthcare adoption. A 
better understanding of these factors will help these stake-
holder groups enhance their services and speed up the adop-
tion of CloudIoT in the healthcare area.

1‚Äā Introduction
Improving the quality of healthcare services is a top pri-
ority for governments and health organizations worldwide. 
Conventional healthcare systems face various challenges, 
including a rise in chronic diseases, an aging population, 
a shortage of medical professionals, and higher healthcare 
costs [1, 2]. As a result, innovative approaches to health-
care delivery are necessary, such as promoting preventative 
behavior and leveraging technology-based healthcare [1].
Generally, using information technology in healthcare is 
termed ‚Äėe-health‚Äô [3]; e-health has many benefits, including 
improved information interchange and healthcare quality [4]. 
However, current e-health systems have several challenges, 
including heterogeneous devices, a lack of processing capac-

space [5]. This provides fertile ground for using cutting-edge 
technologies to develop new e-health systems, like the adop-
tion of CloudIoT technology.

patient‚Äôs vital signs, potentially saving lives. Additionally,

2‚Äā Literature review
This section introduces the CIoTHC paradigm, reviews 
eHealth adoption studies, and summarizes relevant theo-
retical models from existing technology adoption studies 
in various healthcare settings. This robust foundation will 
support our research on health professional‚Äôs acceptance of 
the CIoTHC model.
2.1‚Äā CloudIoT‚ÄĎbased healthcare
New technologies like Cloud Computing (CC) and the 
Internet of Things (IoT) have emerged throughout the ICT 
revolution of the twenty-first century. It‚Äôs important to note 
that these two technologies vary significantly, yet it‚Äôs also 
true that they complement one another [7]. CloudIoT inte-
grates these two technologies, improving their capabilities 
by addressing IoT-related issues such as data access, data 
analysis, security, privacy, and computing process [9].
Experts believe that adopting CIoTHC technology pro-
vides various prospects for medical IT and considerably 
enhances healthcare services [10]. A significant aspect of 
CIoTHC is its ubiquitous, high-quality, and low-cost medi-
cal services. CloudIoT streamlines healthcare operations and 
improves medical service quality by allowing collaboration 
among parties. IoT Sensors create massive amounts of data 
that must be subsequently processed in healthcare systems 
[11]. Meanwhile, CC provides a successful way to manage 
and process sensor-collected data. In addition, CC offers 
flexible storage and processing infrastructure and boosts 
medical data security, privacy, and service availability, 
which are the common issues of IoT [12]. Consequently, 
driven by the effect of CloudIoT in improving healthcare 
services, many CIoTHC systems have been developed and 
presented.
However, little evidence in the literature gives insights 
into CIoTHC adoption via developing a theoretical model 
focusing on user‚Äôs intentions. Indeed, the application of 
technology adoption theories has been explored less in the 
CIoTHC context. The previous studies have focused on the 
systems architectures and the different features of CIoTHC 
services. Nevertheless, end-user‚Äôs adoption of this technol-
ogy has significantly been overlooked in previous research 
studies.
2.2‚Äā Introducing new technology into¬†healthcare
Introducing new technologies in healthcare is a two-
way process where technology influences the healthcare 
organization‚Äôs operations while end-users impact techno-
logical advancements. This makes end-user engagement 
and support vital for successful outcomes [13]. Studies 
have revealed that accepting novel technologies by HCPs 
is crucial for effective implementation. Conversely, if these 
professionals reject new technology, it can lead to project 
failures [14, 15]. For instance, a telemedicine initiative in 
Germany faced a five-year delay due to physicians‚Äô reluc-
tance to embrace the technology despite acknowledging 
its benefits [16]. Similarly, in Seoul, the slow uptake of a 
telemedicine system initiated in 1988 was primarily attrib-
uted to HCP‚Äôs hesitance towards a new eHealth system [17]. 
HCPs often hesitate to adopt new eHealth systems, fearing 
a compromise in healthcare quality.
The successful implementation of new technology in 
healthcare largely depends on HCPs‚Äô acceptance, as shown 
in the cases above. Several studies confirm this finding by 
highlighting the challenges of introducing new technology 
in healthcare settings [18‚Äď21]. Therefore, to implement new 
healthcare technologies effectively, organizations should 
focus not only on technical aspects but also on understanding 
and meeting the needs of HCPs. This approach will ensure 
successful implementation and avoid unnecessary expenses.
2.3‚Äā Review of¬†ehealth adoption studies
The existing research investigates eHealth adoption through 
two primary perspectives: subjects and objects. The sub-
jects of adoption are the individuals or entities considering 
adopting new healthcare services. They can be categorized 
into three groups: healthcare organizations, profession-
als, and recipients. The motivation for adoption may dif-
fer based on the subject. For example, healthcare recipients 
(HCRs) prioritize how the service can positively impact 
their health, while HCPs focus on improving job efficiency 
and performance.
Martins et¬† al. [22] found that patients adoption of 
mHealth is heavily influenced by health concerns and exist-
ing healthcare inefficiencies. On the other hand, Fan et¬†al. 
[21] showed that HCP‚Äôs adoption of AI-based medical diag-
nosis support systems (AIMDSS) is mainly driven by their 
work environment and the technology‚Äôs perceived benefits. 
As for healthcare organizations, they tend to focus on factors 
such as hospital characteristics, government regulations, and 
support programs.
Furthermore, many studies group respondents based on 
specific attributes to gain a better understanding of adoption 
behavior. Gender is commonly used as a moderating vari-
able to investigate gender disparities among HCPs in vari-
ous eHealth technology adoption studies, including those on 
IoT-based healthcare [23], electronic health records (EHRs) 
[24], and mHealth [25].

2.4‚Äā Technology adoption theories
Adapting and integrating theories of technology adoption 
in many sectors assists us in developing a more precise 
understanding of user adoption behavior and the associated 
processes. Many technology adoption theories have been 
frequently applied in healthcare adoption domains, includ-
ing the Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB), Technology 
Acceptance Model (TAM), theory of Diffusion of Innova-
tion (DOI), Technology Organization Environment (TOE), 
Task Technology Fit (TTF), Unified Theory of Acceptance 
and Use of Technology (UTAUT), and UTAUT2. TAM is 
usually utilized to study people‚Äôs behavior toward adopting 
healthcare services. Perceived usefulness and ease of use 
are two key factors in TAM that impact the behavioral inten-
tion that, in turn, affects actual user behavior. The previous 
research studies that used TAM all supposed these relations. 
Papa et¬†al. [29] extended the original TAM with two exter-
nal variables to investigate factors affecting users‚Äô adoption 
of wearable health devices. The findings showed that all 
variables were significant. Another instance was offered by 
Kamal et¬†al. [30], who extended TAM with seven external 
variables (Privacy, Trust, Perceived risk, User resistance, 
Technology anxiety, Facilitating conditions, and Social 
influence) to explore the determinants affecting telemedi-
cine adoption by patients. According to the findings, both 
the original TAM variables and the seven external variables 
significantly impacted the adoption of telemedicine.
TPB postulates that behavioral intention is influenced by 
attitude, subjective norms, and behavioral control. TPB has 
rarely been used alone in healthcare adoption studies; it‚Äôs 
often combined with other theories. For instance, Wu et¬†al. 
[31] integrated TPB with TAM to investigate factors influ-
encing mHealth service adoption by HCPs.
TOE is a well-known theory for analyzing organizations‚Äô 
behavior when deciding whether to accept new services. The 
TOE focuses on three dimensions: technical, organizational, 
and environmental, to investigate the organization‚Äôs adoption 
of new technologies. For example, Yang et¬†al. [32] applied 
TOE to investigate hospitals‚Äô decisions to adopt health infor-
mation systems.
Innovation adoption may be explained by DOI theory 
as a process in which a novel thing is progressively under-
stood and accepted by a particular group over time via a 
specific set of channels. In contrast to other theories, the 
DOI emphasizes the importance of information at every step 
of the adoption process [33]. DOI has been used in health-
care technology adoption studies to investigate individual‚Äôs 
and organization‚Äôs adoption decisions. Yet, it has been uti-
lized more greatly with individuals‚Äô adoption decisions than 
organizations. For instance, DOI was used by Chew et¬†al. 
[34] to explore how to increase Internet use by family doc-
tors. In this study using DOI, the researchers revealed pivotal 
information encouraging doctors to begin and continue using 
the Internet.
According to TTF theory, user adoption behavior can be 
explained by the degree of compatibility between technol-
ogy and the tasks it supports [35]. TPB has not been used 
alone in healthcare adoption studies and is usually combined 
with other theories. For example, Wang et¬†al. [36] combined 
TTF with UTAUT to investigate the adoption of wearable 
health devices. Their findings demonstrated that the interac-
tion between tasks and technology significantly influenced 
consumers‚Äô adoption intentions.
As a unified theory that integrates eight outstanding 
theories of technology adoption, UTAUT has recently 
been popular in the literature on healthcare service adop-
tion. UTAUT was first suggested in 2003 by Venkatesh 
et¬†al. [37]. Even though it came out later than other theo-
ries, it has a higher support level than others. An instance

3‚Äā Theoretical model and¬†hypotheses
In recent decades, many models have been developed to 
study information systems‚Äô acceptance and use intention. 
Studies on these models found that the best theoretical basis 
for the customer‚Äôs perspective should be considered [41]. 
Therefore, the UTAUT2 model was selected as a theoreti-
cal foundation for its higher predictive potential, especially 
relevant in the user‚Äôs context, to find an appropriate theory 
encompassing practically all factors impacting the adoption 
intention of CIoTHC. However, UTAUT2 constructs are 
insufficient, and the model should be modified or extended 
when applied in the healthcare domain, as suggested by 
many researchers [38, 39]. Therefore, the UTAUT2 model is

‚ÄúFacilitating conditions (FCs) refers to the degree to which

evaluating the influence of FCs in the adoption of different 
technology-based healthcare services found that FCs had 
a positive impact on adoption intention [39, 48, 49]. Like-
wise, HCPs are more likely to embrace CIoTHC if they have 
enough organizational and technical infrastructure. Based on 
the above, the following hypothesis is formulated:
H4: FCs significantly influence HCPs‚Äô behavioral inten-
tion to adopt CIoTHC.
3.6‚Äā Habit
‚ÄúHabit (HB) is an outcome of experience using and practic-

habits are shifting due to the introduction of cutting-edge 
technology [50]. According to previous research on tech-
nology adoption, habitual use helps people get more famil-
iar with a product by dispelling common misunderstand-
ings [51]. Many research studies have determined habit as 
a significant factor in healthcare technology adoption. For 
example, Kwateng et¬†al. [45] investigated factors influenc-
ing health information systems adoption among HCPs in 
Ghana. The findings showed the significant effects of habit 
on HCP‚Äôs behavioral intention. Based on the above, the fol-
lowing hypothesis is formulated:
H5: HB significantly influences HCP‚Äôs behavioral inten-
tion to adopt CIoTHC.
3.7‚Äā Trust
‚ÄúTrust (TR) refers to the willingness of an individual to 
depend on a partner to whom he has confidence‚ÄĚ [52]. TR

practitioners, and scholars have all agreed that trust in the 
digital era is beneficial [52]. E-Health‚Äôs acceptability relies 
heavily on user trust, which is highlighted as the essential 
factor in healthcare technology adoption literature [31]. For 
example, Fan et¬†al. [21] investigated factors influencing the

showed the significant effects of trust on the HCP‚Äôs behavio-
ral intention. For CIoTHC services, trust in data analysis and 
monitoring would significantly impact adoption. Therefore, 
the following hypothesis is formulated:
H6: TR significantly influences HCP‚Äôs behavioral inten-
tion to adopt CIoTHC.

3.10‚Äā User innovativeness
‚ÄúUser Innovativeness (UI) is defined as an individual‚Äôs

3.11‚Äā Task complexity
‚ÄúTask complexity (TC) is the degree of perceived difficulty

with varying complexity levels. HCPs may perceive the 
more significant benefit of CloudIoT for more sophisticated 
tasks (ongoing monitoring and analysis of patient data) than 
more straightforward tasks. Thus, we argue that TC is asso-
ciated with adoption intention in the context of CIoTHC, and 
we propose the following hypothesis:
H10: TC significantly influences HCP‚Äôs behavioral inten-
tion to adopt CIoTHC.
4‚Äā Research design and¬†methods
Our methodology follows a structured multi-phase approach 
that outlines the sequential steps taken during the study. 
The approach starts with an initial review of the literature 
on the CloudIoT paradigm, theories, models, and eHealth 
adoption studies. This is followed by model development, 
which includes hypothesis formulation, model construction, 
and a pilot study for preliminary validation. The next phase 
involves data collection and model validation, where we use 
a three-step SEM and BN approach to examine the gathered 
data. Finally, the results and discussion phase interprets find-
ings, highlights contributions to the field, and draws conclu-
sions. Figure¬†3 shows the structured multi-phase approach 
for our methodology.

5‚Äā Data analysis
The data analysis is performed using SEM and BN

6‚Äā Discussion
An empirical study was performed utilizing an extending 
UTAUT2 to understand factors influencing HCP‚Äôs behavio-
ral intention to adopt CIoTHC in the Jordanian context. In 
addition to the UTAUT2 model‚Äôs factors, five vital factors 
(TR, PR, SC, UI, and TC) were also investigated. A theo-
retical model was proposed and tested in three steps. First, 
SEM was utilized to conduct the path analysis and test the 
postulated hypotheses. Second, the significant factors pro-
duced by SEM were entered into the BN as inputs to predict 
the relations among these factors. Third, the SEM was used 
to analyze the modified model‚Äôs causal relations resulting 
from steps 1 and 2.
This research‚Äôs findings somewhat align with the existing 
literature on the UTAUT2 application in healthcare. The R2 
for BI was 0.827, which is deemed strong compared to the 
reference value of the BI (52%) in the original UTAUT2 
model [73], showing that the proposed model has more pre-
dictive power than the original UTAUT2 model that predicts 
0.52 of the variation in behavioral intention [38]. Thus, the 
research findings showed the good predictive power of the 
proposed model for predicting HCP‚Äôs intentions to adopt 
CIoTHC.
The first step of the analysis showed that PE had a sig-
nificant effect on BI in the context of CIoTHC, which is 
consistent with existing literature in the technology-based 
healthcare adoption domain, such as UTAUT [74, 75], 
UTAUT2 [22, 76] and TAM [29, 77]. Consequently, HCPs 
who believe that CloudIoT would be helpful and productive 
in their work and can improve conventional healthcare sys-
tems are likelier to adopt such technology. The result also 
showed that EE significantly affected BI, consistent with 
existing literature on technology-based healthcare adoption 
[78, 79] and the original UTAUT2 model [38]. Therefore, 
HCPs who perceive the easiness of using CIoTHC are more 
likely to adopt it. Besides, SI was shown not significantly 
to influence BI in the CIoTHC adoption context. This result 
contradicts the current literature in the technology-based 
healthcare domain [80, 81], and the original UTAUT2 
model outcome [38]. This result might be due to the sensi-
tivity of the healthcare process, where HCPs are concerned 
about their patient‚Äôs lives and can‚Äôt use technology based 
on others‚Äô opinions. In addition, FCs positively affected BI, 
implying that HCPs need technical infrastructure availability 
while heading toward BI. This result is consistent with the 
results of previous literature on technology-based healthcare

Moreover, the result confirmed that HB had a statisti-

studies in the e-health domain [45, 76]. Habit is more than 
just repeating one‚Äôs previous actions and routines; it may 
also contain qualities such as unintentionality and uncon-

their behavior towards technology are more likely to adopt 
CIoTHC. Further, the result indicated that SE and PR sig-
nificantly influenced the BI, consistent with the previous 
e-health adoption literature [39, 57]. Therefore, HCP‚Äôs per-
ceptions of how safe and private the use of CloudIoT sys-
tems and means affect their adoption intention. The result 
also revealed that TR significantly impacted BI, which is 
consistent with current literature on the e-health domain. 
TR has been tested and confirmed as a prerequisite for end-
user‚Äôs acceptance of e-health in various empirical studies 
as an extension to several well-known technology accept-
ance theories, such as TAM [83, 84], UTAUT [19, 85], and 
UTAUT2 [39, 54]. Thus, HCPs‚Äô acceptance of CIoTHC 
depends on their confidence in such technology. Finally, 
our results reveal that TC and UI were not associated with 
BI, which is inconsistent with the existing literature on the 
technology-based healthcare adoption domain [21, 24]. This 
result might be because HCPs are more cautious when deter-
mining whether to embrace specific technologies to protect 
the quality of healthcare services.
During the second step, the BN analysis revealed four 
new relationships among variables not considered in the 
proposed model, including the influence of EE on PE and 
the impacts of PR, FCs, and PE on TR. Incorporating these 
relationships into the proposed model revealed the role of 
TR in mediating the effect of PR, FCs, and PE on BI. Moreo-
ver, it showed that PE mediates the relationship between 
EE and BI. Thus, the resulting model was analyzed using 
SEM in the third step to statistically test and verify these 
relationships.
In addition to the results of step one, the third analysis 
step demonstrated EE‚Äôs significant influence on PE, congru-
ent with the existing e-health adoption literature [21, 86]. 
Consequently, a lower effort perception leads to a higher 
performance expectancy. The results also indicated that TR

7‚Äā Theoretical implications
Although many research studies have studied HCP‚Äôs adop-
tion of several systems and technologies, they haven‚Äôt 
addressed the CIoTHC adoption. Therefore, this research 
study investigated HCP‚Äôs behavioral intention to adopt 
CIoTHC. Thus, a new adoption object was added to the 
research area. Besides, by extending UTAUT2 with three 
additional factors, our study contributed to the technology-
based healthcare adoption area. More factors were added 
to UTAUT2, and new causal relations among variables 
were predicted by BN and verified by SEM in this empiri-
cal study, which goes beyond Venkatesh et¬†al. [73] initial 
UTAUT2 model. In addition, this research adds to the IS 
adoption literature by introducing a new three-step approach 
to predicting HCP‚Äôs adoption of CIoTHC, which combines 
the benefits of BN and SEM approaches. Moreover, this 
research yielded credible results that may be generalized to 
the target population. Also, it developed the current adop-
tion research model, revealed the significant mediating role 
of TR, and explored the main factors that influence TR and 
BI in the CIoTHC context.
8‚Äā Practical implications
Several practical implications are inferred from this empiri-
cal study to aid stakeholder groups in better understand-
ing the factors supporting the adoption of CIoTHC. This 
research study shows that TR is the most statistically sig-
nificant determinant of CloudIoT adoption among HCPs, 
meaning enhancing trust in CIoTHC would attract HCPs. 
Besides, designers and developers can use these research 
results to increase the adoption of CIoTHC by integrating 
the factors contributing to the development of trust (per-
formance expectancy, privacy, and facilitating conditions) 
in the design and development phases. CIoTHC features 
and functionalities (PE) play an essential role in HCPs‚Äô 
behavioral intentions. Thus, healthcare providers, develop-
ers, and designers should pay close attention to the features 
and functionalities they offer in their systems and how they 
may be tailored to meet the demands of various HCPs. In 
addition, given the cautiousness of HCPs about healthcare 
quality, FCs should be taken into consideration, where HCPs 
perceive the availability of such resources influences their 
behavioral intention.
Moreover, because of the sensitive nature of data stored 
and processed in CIoTHC, SE and PR significantly impact 
how HCPs perceive CIoTHC and, thus, their behavioral 
intentions. Developers and designers should pay special 
attention to the SE and PR measures included in the sys-
tems and ensure that HCPs are aware of these measures to 
ensure the data is safe and secure. Developers and designers 
should strike a balance between adding new features and 
ensuring the safety and security of the data. Besides, EE has 
a significant impact on the behavioral intentions of HCPs. 
Thus, it‚Äôs vital to design and develop CIoTHC systems that 
are simple and easy to use. Therefore, we propose that HCPs 
test a pilot application of the system to make adjustments 
during the development stage and boost the system‚Äôs accept-
ance. Furthermore, since habit is repeating one‚Äôs previous 
actions and routines because of learning, CIoTHC systems 
must provide customer support services to help and motivate 
HCPs to accept and use such systems.
9‚Äā Conclusion
In the healthcare industry 4.0, CIoTHC is one of the newest 
advancements in the technology healthcare environment dur-
ing the last years. Although the significant benefits offered 
by CIoTHC, its adoption rate is still low among HCPs. Thus, 
a research initiative to identify the critical factors driving 
the behavioral intention toward CIoTHC is essential. To this 
end, we proposed an extended UTAUT2 model to deter-
mine the significant factors affecting the HCP‚Äôs adoption of 
CIoTHC. Thus, we contribute to the body of knowledge on 
CIoTHC adoption in three ways:
1.	 This research is the first effort to offer beneficial insights 
into adopting CIoTHC in Jordan, a developing country.
2.	 Previous research studies have significantly overlooked 
end-user adoption of CIoTHC solutions. Thus, develop-
ing a theoretical adoption model, which is the primary 
contribution of this research, is deemed a beneficial tool.
3.	 Another contribution of this research in the informa-
tion systems adoption literature is introducing a new 
three-step strategy in predicting HCP‚Äôs adoption of 
CIoTHC, which combines the benefits of BN and SEM 
approaches.
Moreover, this research study confirms the roles of PE, 
EE, FCs, HB, TR, PR, and SE in technology-based health-
care adoption. SEM was utilized in the first analysis step to 
conduct the path analysis and test the postulated hypotheses. 
In the second analysis step, the significant factors produced 
by SEM were entered into the BN as inputs to predict the 
relations among these factors. In the third step, the SEM was 
used to analyze the causal relations of the modified model 
that resulted from steps 1 and 2.

10‚Äā Limitation and¬†future work
One limitation of our study is that we did not investigate 
the actual usage of CIoTHC. Future research should inves-
tigate actual use to gain a more comprehensive understand-
ing of the adoption process. Additionally, our study only 
considered gender as a moderator variable among the fac-
tors. Future research could explore other variables, such as 
professional rank and age, which might impact the findings. 
Furthermore, the scope of our study was limited to Jordan. 
As CIoTHC adoption is a global phenomenon, it is crucial to 
replicate the study in diverse geographical contexts. Lastly, 
our research employed an online survey, and participants 
were selected using a combination of snowball and conveni-
ence sampling techniques. Therefore, future research can 
employ systematic and random-based sampling techniques 
to enhance the generalizability of the research model.
